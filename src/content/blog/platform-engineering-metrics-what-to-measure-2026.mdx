# Platform Engineering Metrics: What to Measure & Why 2026

Platform engineering teams face a unique challenge: demonstrating value. Unlike application development where you ship a feature and users react, platform work is often invisible when it's working well. Developers use the CI/CD pipeline, provision databases, and deploy services — and if the platform is excellent, they don't think about the platform at all.

This invisibility makes metrics critical. Without data, platform engineering gets cut in budget cycles ("what does the platform team actually produce?"). With data, you can show exactly how the platform reduces toil, accelerates delivery, and improves developer satisfaction.

This guide covers the metrics that matter, how to collect them, and how to use them to drive platform improvements.

## The Four Categories of Platform Engineering Metrics

Platform engineering metrics fall into four categories:

1. **Delivery performance** (DORA metrics) — How fast and reliably does software ship?
2. **Platform adoption** — Are developers using the platform?
3. **Developer experience** — Do developers feel more productive?
4. **Operational efficiency** — Is the platform reducing toil?

You need metrics from all four categories. Focus only on DORA metrics and you miss adoption problems. Focus only on adoption and you miss whether the platform is actually improving developer experience.

## Category 1: DORA Metrics

The DORA (DevOps Research and Assessment) four key metrics are the industry standard for software delivery performance. Platform engineering should move these metrics.

### Deployment Frequency

**What it measures**: How often a team successfully deploys to production.

**Why it matters**: High deployment frequency (multiple times per day) indicates low-risk, automated deployments. Platform golden paths for CI/CD directly enable higher deployment frequency.

**How to collect**: Query your deployment system (Argo CD, Spinnaker, GitHub Actions) for production deployments per team per day/week.

**Benchmarks (DORA 2024):**
- Elite: Multiple times per day
- High: Between once per day and once per week
- Medium: Between once per week and once per month
- Low: Less than once per month or as needed

**Platform connection**: A well-built golden path CI/CD pipeline reduces deployment risk and friction, enabling teams to deploy more frequently. If deployment frequency is low, your pipeline is likely too complex or risky.

### Lead Time for Changes

**What it measures**: Time from code committed to code running in production.

**Why it matters**: Long lead times mean slow feedback and slow value delivery. Platform CI/CD, approval workflows, and deployment automation directly control this metric.

**How to collect**: Track time from the first commit in a PR to successful production deployment. Most CI systems can emit this data; GitHub Actions and Argo CD both have APIs.

**Benchmarks:**
- Elite: Less than one hour
- High: Between one day and one week
- Medium: Between one week and one month
- Low: Between one month and six months

**Platform connection**: Every manual gate in your deployment pipeline adds lead time. If lead time is high, look for manual approval steps, long-running test suites, or serial vs parallel pipeline stages.

### Change Failure Rate

**What it measures**: Percentage of deployments that cause a production incident (rollback, hotfix, or outage).

**Why it matters**: A high change failure rate means deployments are risky. Platform responsibilities include automated testing in CI, canary deployments, and rollback automation.

**How to collect**: Track deployments vs incidents correlated to recent deployments (usually within 1 hour of deploy). Your incident management system (PagerDuty, OpsGenie) + deployment system combined.

**Benchmarks:**
- Elite: 0–15%
- High: 16–30%
- Medium: 31–45%
- Low: 46–60%

**Platform connection**: Good CI pipelines with thorough test coverage reduce change failure rate. Canary deployments and feature flags reduce the blast radius when failures do occur.

### Time to Restore Service (MTTR)

**What it measures**: How long it takes to recover from a production incident.

**Why it matters**: Incidents are inevitable. Fast recovery minimizes user impact. Platform responsibilities include runbooks, observability tooling, and deployment rollback capabilities.

**How to collect**: Measure time from incident creation to resolution in your incident management system.

**Benchmarks:**
- Elite: Less than one hour
- High: Less than one day
- Medium: Between one day and one week
- Low: Between one week and one month

**Platform connection**: Good observability (fast incident diagnosis) and reliable rollback capabilities (fast remediation) are platform responsibilities that directly reduce MTTR.

## Category 2: Platform Adoption Metrics

DORA metrics tell you about delivery performance but not whether the platform is the cause. Adoption metrics close this gap.

### Golden Path Adoption Rate

**What it measures**: Percentage of new services/deployments using platform golden paths.

**Formula**: `(Services using golden path / Total new services) × 100`

**Target**: 80%+ for new services. The goal isn't 100% — some services will always have legitimate reasons to deviate.

**How to collect**: Check `catalog-info.yaml` annotations for template version field, or track via Backstage/Port metadata.

### Self-Service Action Success Rate

**What it measures**: Percentage of self-service requests (database provisioning, secret creation, etc.) that complete successfully without human intervention.

**Formula**: `(Successful self-service completions / Total self-service requests) × 100`

**Target**: 95%+. If self-service fails frequently, developers will route around it via tickets.

**How to collect**: Instrument your self-service workflows (Backstage Scaffolder logs, Port action logs, Terraform Cloud run results).

### Developer Portal DAU/WAU

**What it measures**: Daily and weekly active users of the developer portal.

**Why it matters**: A portal that nobody uses doesn't reduce cognitive load.

**Target**: 60%+ of engineers using the portal at least weekly.

**How to collect**: Web analytics (Plausible, PostHog, or Backstage's built-in analytics).

### Catalog Completeness Score

**What it measures**: Percentage of production services with complete catalog metadata.

**Formula**: `(Services with owner + docs + monitoring + on-call / Total services) × 100`

**Target**: 95%+ for production services.

**How to collect**: Query your catalog API and check required fields. Backstage TechInsights plugin can automate this. Port and Cortex have built-in scorecard capabilities.

### Time to First Deployment (New Engineers)

**What it measures**: Time from a new engineer joining to their first production deployment.

**Why it matters**: This is the most concrete measure of onboarding experience, which platform tooling directly controls.

**Target**: < 1 day (with golden paths). Baseline without platform investment is often 5–10 days.

**How to collect**: Track in your HR system (hire date) vs deployment system (first deploy). Or use a new-hire survey.

## Category 3: Developer Experience Metrics

Developer satisfaction is the ultimate outcome. Technical metrics are proxies — developer experience metrics measure whether the platform is actually making developers feel more productive.

### Developer NPS (Net Promoter Score)

**What it measures**: "On a scale of 0-10, how likely are you to recommend working at [company] to a friend, specifically because of our developer tools and infrastructure?"

**Formula**: `% Promoters (9-10) - % Detractors (0-6)`

**Benchmark**: +20 to +30 is healthy for developer experience. +50 is excellent.

**How to collect**: Quarterly survey via Google Forms, TypeForm, or a dedicated DevEx survey tool.

**Platform connection**: NPS integrates everything — is the portal useful? Are golden paths effective? Does CI/CD feel reliable? One number that captures the whole developer experience.

### Developer Experience Survey — Specific Dimensions

Supplement NPS with specific dimensions (1-5 scale):

```
In the past month, rate your experience with:

Infrastructure provisioning:   1 2 3 4 5
CI/CD pipeline reliability:   1 2 3 4 5
Developer portal usefulness:  1 2 3 4 5
Documentation quality:        1 2 3 4 5
Onboarding experience:        1 2 3 4 5
Time spent on infrastructure: 1 2 3 4 5
```

Specific dimensions help identify exactly where to improve.

### Time Spent on Infrastructure Tasks

**What it measures**: Percentage of work time developers spend on infrastructure vs product work.

**Target**: < 10% on infrastructure for most application developers. The platform should absorb the rest.

**How to collect**: Time tracking survey, or ask in developer survey "approximately what % of your time last week was infrastructure/tooling vs product work?"

**Benchmark**: Without a platform, typical answer is 20–40%. With a mature platform, 5–10%.

## Category 4: Operational Efficiency Metrics

These metrics show the platform team's efficiency and the reduction in ops toil.

### Infrastructure Ticket Volume

**What it measures**: Number of tickets filed to the ops/platform team for things that should be self-service.

**Target**: Decreasing trend. If developers can provision databases via self-service, database provisioning tickets should approach zero.

**How to collect**: Jira/Linear ticket analysis by label or assignee.

**Platform connection**: Every self-service capability you ship should reduce a category of tickets. Track the before/after.

### Platform Incident Rate

**What it measures**: Number of incidents caused by platform infrastructure (not application code).

**Target**: Fewer than 2 platform-caused incidents per month.

**How to collect**: Tag incidents in your incident management system by root cause.

### On-Call Burden (Platform Team)

**What it measures**: Average interruptions per on-call shift for the platform team.

**Target**: < 2 interruptions per shift. High on-call burden indicates reliability problems.

**How to collect**: PagerDuty/OpsGenie alert volume analysis.

### Mean Time to Platform Service Delivery

**What it measures**: How long it takes from a team requesting a new platform capability to it being available.

**Target**: < 2 weeks for standard requests.

**How to collect**: Track platform team issues/tickets from creation to delivery.

## Building a Platform Metrics Dashboard

### The Weekly Platform Review (15 minutes)

Every week, the platform team reviews:

```
📊 Platform Health Dashboard — Week of Apr 7

DORA Metrics (company-wide)
  Deployment Frequency:  2.3/day (+0.4 WoW) ✅
  Lead Time:             4.2 hours (-0.8 WoW) ✅
  Change Failure Rate:   8% (same WoW) ✅
  MTTR:                  42 min (-15 min WoW) ✅

Platform Adoption
  Golden Path Adoption:  76% (+3% MoM)
  Portal DAU:            87 users (+5 WoW)
  Catalog Completeness:  82% (+1% WoW) ⚠️
  Self-Service Success:  94% (-1% WoW) ⚠️

Developer Experience
  Last NPS (Q1):         +28
  Infra Tickets Filed:   12 (-3 WoW) ✅

Platform Health
  Platform Incidents:    1 this week
  On-call Alerts:        3 (low severity)
```

### Monthly Platform Scorecard

Monthly: aggregate metrics, trend analysis, identify improvement priorities.

### Quarterly Developer Survey

Quarterly: full developer experience survey, NPS + specific dimensions, share results with entire engineering org.

## Using Metrics to Prioritize Platform Work

The best use of platform metrics is prioritization. Before committing to platform work, ask:

1. **Which DORA metric is weakest?** If change failure rate is high, invest in testing/canary. If lead time is high, invest in pipeline optimization.

2. **Which adoption metric is low?** If golden path adoption is 40%, build better onboarding and promotion. If portal DAU is low, investigate whether the portal solves real problems.

3. **What does the developer survey say?** The lowest-scoring dimension in the quarterly survey should inform next quarter's platform roadmap.

4. **Which ticket category is largest?** The infrastructure ticket category with the most volume is a self-service opportunity.

## Common Metric Pitfalls

### Vanity Metrics

Metrics that look good but don't indicate real value:
- **Deployment count** (without deployment success rate)
- **Portal page views** (without repeat usage)
- **Number of golden paths** (without adoption rate)

### Goodhart's Law in Platform Engineering

"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."

If you tell teams they'll be evaluated on deployment frequency, they'll deploy small meaningless changes to inflate the metric. Frame metrics as diagnostic tools, not performance scorecards.

### Missing Baseline

Always measure before you build, not just after. A 20% improvement sounds good until you realize the baseline was already excellent.

## Conclusion

Platform engineering metrics serve two purposes: proving value to leadership and guiding the team's improvement efforts.

The DORA four metrics give you delivery performance. Adoption metrics tell you whether developers use what you build. Developer experience metrics tell you whether it actually helps. Operational metrics tell you whether you're reducing toil.

Used together, these metrics transform platform engineering from a "trust me it's valuable" function to a data-driven discipline with clear ROI. And in 2026, when engineering budgets are scrutinized more closely than ever, that data is what keeps platform teams funded and growing.

Start with DORA metrics and a quarterly developer survey. Add adoption metrics as you build more platform capabilities. The goal is a feedback loop: measure → build → measure → improve.
